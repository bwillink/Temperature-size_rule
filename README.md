# Temperature-size_rule

Developmental temperature determines adult body size in many taxa. The Temperature-Size Rule (TSR) is a frequently observed pattern where the final size of organisms reared in warmer conditions is smaller than that of individuals reared in the cold. The Differential Development and Growth (DDG) rate hypothesis is a mechanistic explanation for the TSR that states that thermal plasticity in size at maturity results from temperature impacting the rate of development more strongly than the rate of growth. Here we measured the effect of temperature on the growth trajectories of eight species of New Zealand stick insect from across a known phylogeny. We calculated the thermal sensitivity of growth and development rate to see whether the size at maturity follows the DDG predictions. We found limited support for the DDG hypothesis. Rearing temperature determined growth, development, and adult body size as predicted by the DDG hypothesis in only two species (both in the genus Acanthoxyla). Furthermore, phylogenetic comparative analysis indicated that TSR evolved once within New Zealand stick insects, in a shared ancestor of these two species. Thus, the TSR is a derived trait that likely has a multicausal explanation, rather than being an inevitable consequence of temperature effects on the growth and development of ectotherms.

Extant-species estimates and ancestral state reconstructions for **(a)** the thermal-sensitivity differential of development and growth rates and **(b)** the thermal-sensitivity outcome for final mass – i.e. whether mass differences between cold-acclimated and warm-acclimated individuals follow the temperature-size rule (TSR).
